Yosemite National Park

Experience one of America's most breathtaking natural treasures with towering granite monoliths, spectacular waterfalls, and ancient sequoia groves in California's Sierra Nevada mountains.

About Yosemite

Yosemite National Park, spanning nearly 1,200 square miles in California's Sierra Nevada mountains, stands as one of America's most iconic natural landscapes. Established in 1890 as the nation's third national park, Yosemite played a pivotal role in the national park movement, inspired largely by the advocacy of naturalist John Muir. The park's dramatic terrain was sculpted by glacial action over millions of years, creating the distinctive U-shaped Yosemite Valley with its sheer granite walls rising over 3,000 feet from the valley floor. This geological wonderland features some of North America's tallest waterfalls, including the 2,425-foot Yosemite Falls and the striking Bridalveil Fall, whose mists were believed by the native Ahwahneechee people to increase fertility. Yosemite's remarkable biodiversity encompasses five major vegetation zones supporting over 250 species of vertebrates and thousands of plant species, including ancient giant sequoia trees in Mariposa Grove that have stood for over 3,000 years. Iconic landmarks like Half Dome, El Capitan, and Glacier Point draw millions of visitors annually, while also challenging world-class rock climbers on their vertical faces. Beyond the popular valley, which represents just 1% of the park's area, Yosemite encompasses vast wilderness regions with subalpine meadows, pristine lakes, and 800 miles of hiking trails. The park's cultural history dates back thousands of years through the indigenous Ahwahneechee people, followed by the Gold Rush era and the eventual conservation movement that recognized its extraordinary natural value. Today, Yosemite welcomes approximately 4 million visitors annually while balancing accessibility with preservation of its ecological integrity for future generations.

Top Experiences

Yosemite Falls

Marvel at North America's tallest waterfall dropping 2,425 feet in three dramatic sections, with easy viewing from Yosemite Valley or challenging hiking trails to its upper sections.

Half Dome

Admire this iconic granite dome from Glacier Point or tackle the legendary 14-16 mile round-trip hike with cable assistance to its 8,800-foot summit (permit required).

Tunnel View

Capture the classic panoramic vista of Yosemite Valley featuring El Capitan, Half Dome, and Bridalveil Fall from this easily accessible viewpoint near Wawona Tunnel.

Mariposa Grove

Walk among over 500 mature giant sequoias, including the 1,800-year-old Grizzly Giant, on accessible trails through this ancient forest in the southern section of the park.

Travel Tips

Essential information for visiting Yosemite

When to Visit

Each season offers distinct Yosemite experiences. Summer (June-August) brings peak crowds but optimal conditions for hiking, climbing, and full access to all park areas including high country roads and trails. Spring (April-May) features spectacular waterfalls at maximum flow as snow melts, though some high-elevation areas remain inaccessible. Fall (September-October) offers smaller crowds, pleasant weather, and beautiful autumn colors, especially among black oaks and dogwoods, though waterfalls may be reduced or dry. Winter (November-March) transforms the park into a serene snow-covered landscape with dramatically reduced crowds, though tire chains are often required and many facilities close, including Tioga and Glacier Point Roads. For avoiding crowds, consider visiting midweek rather than weekends, and arrive at popular sites before 9am or after 5pm. If visiting during peak summer season, secure lodging or campground reservations 5-6 months in advance, and expect traffic congestion in Yosemite Valley.

Getting Around

Yosemite covers a vast area with several distinct regions. Most visitors focus on Yosemite Valley, the 7-square-mile area containing famous landmarks like Half Dome and Yosemite Falls. Free shuttle buses operate year-round within the valley, connecting visitor centers, lodging, and trailheads—using these shuttles is highly recommended as parking is limited. During summer, additional shuttles serve Glacier Point, Tuolumne Meadows, and Mariposa Grove. For exploring beyond shuttle routes, a personal vehicle provides flexibility, though parking can be challenging during peak times (before 9am arrival recommended). The YARTS bus service connects gateway communities to Yosemite Valley and includes park admission in its fare. If driving, fill your gas tank before entering the park as fuel inside is limited and expensive. Park roads vary seasonally—Tioga Road (Highway 120 through high country) typically closes November through late May, while Glacier Point Road closes during winter. Download offline maps as cell service is very limited throughout the park. For accessibility information, the visitor center provides details on wheelchair-friendly trails and facilities.

Hiking & Safety

Yosemite offers over 800 miles of trails ranging from easy valley floor walks to strenuous climbs. Popular beginner-friendly options include the 1-mile paved Lower Yosemite Falls trail, 2-mile round-trip Bridalveil Fall path, and the 2.2-mile Cook's Meadow Loop offering spectacular valley views. For moderate day hikes, consider the 4.8-mile round-trip Mist Trail to Vernal Fall or the 4.7-mile Valley Loop Trail. Challenging adventures include the 7.2-mile round-trip Upper Yosemite Falls trail (2,700-foot elevation gain) and the 14-16 mile Half Dome hike (permit required). For safety, carry plenty of water (minimum 1 liter per person for short hikes, 3-4 liters for longer treks), wear proper footwear, and pack the "Ten Essentials" including navigation tools, sun protection, first aid supplies, and extra food. Wildlife encounters require maintaining safe distances—stay at least 50 yards from black bears and never feed any animals. Weather changes rapidly in the mountains; bring layers regardless of season and check forecasts before heading out. During winter, traction devices for shoes are recommended even on valley trails. Cell phone coverage is unreliable throughout the park, so inform someone of your hiking plans. Swimming in rivers and lakes requires caution due to strong currents and cold temperatures.

Accommodation

Staying inside Yosemite enhances your experience by reducing commute times and allowing early/late access to popular sites. In-park options include the historic Ahwahnee Hotel (luxury, $500-1000/night), Yosemite Valley Lodge (moderate, $250-350/night), and Curry Village's canvas tent cabins or wood cabins ($120-250/night). The Wawona Hotel offers a historic mountain lodge experience in the southern park area. For camping, Yosemite maintains 13 campgrounds with varying amenities and seasons—North Pines, Upper Pines, and Lower Pines in the valley are most popular, requiring reservations secured through recreation.gov exactly 5 months in advance (released at 7am PT on the 15th of each month and filling within minutes). First-come, first-served options exist at Camp 4 (valley) and several high-country sites, though lines form early. Gateway communities provide additional accommodations, with Mariposa, Oakhurst, and El Portal (closest at 30 minutes from the valley) offering numerous hotels and vacation rentals. During peak summer season, book any accommodation at least 6 months in advance. For those seeking more space or traveling with groups, vacation homes in communities like Fish Camp, Wawona, and Yosemite West provide kitchen facilities and multiple bedrooms, often at lower per-person costs than hotels.

Yosemite FAQ

These questions are the most asked questions and are answerd here.

How many days should I spend in Yosemite?

For first-time visitors, 2-3 days allows time to explore Yosemite Valley's highlights, including iconic viewpoints and a few moderate hikes. With 3-4 days, add Glacier Point, Mariposa Grove, and more extensive hiking. A full week enables exploration of the less-visited high country areas like Tuolumne Meadows and Hetch Hetchy. One day is possible but extremely rushed, limiting you to quick viewpoint stops and perhaps one short hike. Plan to stay as close to the valley as possible to maximize your time, as driving from gateway towns adds 1-2 hours each way.

Do I need a reservation to enter Yosemite?

Yosemite's reservation requirements vary by season and year. During peak summer months, temporary reservation systems have been implemented in recent years to manage visitation (check nps.gov/yose for current status). The $35 per vehicle entrance fee is valid for 7 days. Reservations are not needed if you have lodging or camping booked inside the park, hold a wilderness permit, or arrive via YARTS bus. If reservations are required during your visit, secure them on recreation.gov up to 3 months in advance, as day-use allocations typically sell out quickly. Consider purchasing an $80 America the Beautiful annual pass if visiting multiple national parks.

What are the must-see attractions for first-time visitors?

First-time visitors should prioritize Tunnel View for the classic valley panorama, Glacier Point for sweeping vistas (seasonal road closure in winter), Yosemite Falls (peak flow April-June), and either the Mist Trail to Vernal Fall or the easier Lower Yosemite Falls trail. The 1-2 hour Valley Floor Loop offers excellent views of El Capitan and Half Dome. If time permits, add Mariposa Grove of Giant Sequoias (southern park entrance) and Olmsted Point along Tioga Road (summer/fall only). For photographers, sunrise at Valley View and sunset at Sentinel Bridge (Half Dome reflection) offer iconic shots. Avoid trying to see everything—Yosemite rewards a slower pace with time to appreciate its grandeur.

What's the best area to stay in or near Yosemite?

Staying inside Yosemite Valley provides the best experience with early/late access to attractions and no daily commute. Options range from the upscale Ahwahnee Hotel to more affordable Curry Village tent cabins and campgrounds. Just outside the park, El Portal (west entrance) offers accommodations 30 minutes from the valley. Budget-friendly options exist in Mariposa (45 minutes) and Oakhurst (90 minutes). For southern attractions like Mariposa Grove, Fish Camp and Wawona provide convenient access. The eastern entrance near Lee Vining offers beautiful scenery but a longer drive to the valley (75+ minutes). Book accommodations 4-6 months in advance for summer visits, as options fill quickly, especially for in-park lodging.

What's the best time of year to see the waterfalls?

Yosemite's waterfalls are most spectacular during spring snowmelt, typically late April through June, when falls reach peak flow. By late summer (August-September), many falls including Yosemite Falls may dry up completely or reduce to a trickle. Bridalveil Fall and Vernal Fall typically flow year-round, though at reduced volume in late summer and fall. Winter storms can temporarily rejuvenate some falls. For the ideal combination of flowing waterfalls and accessible trails, late May to early June offers the perfect balance, though with increased crowds. Photography enthusiasts should visit in late February for the annual "Firefall" phenomenon when Horsetail Fall catches sunset light, appearing to glow like flowing lava.

Are there easy hikes suitable for families or beginners?

Yosemite offers numerous family-friendly trails. In the valley, the paved 1-mile Lower Yosemite Falls loop takes about 30 minutes with minimal elevation change and spectacular views. The 2-mile round-trip Bridalveil Fall trail is another easy option. Cook's Meadow Loop (2.2 miles) provides stellar valley views with minimal elevation gain. In the southern park area, Mariposa Grove's Big Trees Loop (0.3 miles) features accessible boardwalks among giant sequoias, while the slightly longer Grizzly Giant Loop (2 miles) reaches the iconic 1,800-year-old Grizzly Giant sequoia. For children, the ranger-led Junior Ranger programs offer educational activities and a badge upon completion. The shuttle system makes these trails easily accessible without navigation concerns.

What should I pack for a Yosemite trip?

Pack with Yosemite's variable mountain weather in mind. Essential items include hiking boots/shoes with good traction, layered clothing (temperatures can fluctuate 30°F in a day), rain jacket (afternoon thunderstorms are common in summer), sun protection (hat, sunscreen, sunglasses), refillable water bottles (2-3 liters per person), basic first aid kit, and insect repellent. A day pack for hiking essentials, binoculars for wildlife viewing, and headlamp/flashlight are highly recommended. During summer, pack a swimsuit for river dips, while winter visits require warm layers, waterproof boots, and potentially traction devices for snow. Bring a physical map as cell service is minimal. Food and basic supplies are available in the park but at premium prices. For accommodations, check if bedding is provided (particularly for Curry Village tent cabins, which offer only beds).

How can I avoid the crowds in Yosemite?

To avoid Yosemite's crowds, timing is everything. Visit during shoulder seasons (April-May or September-October) for fewer visitors while maintaining good access to most areas. If visiting in summer, explore before 9am or after 4pm when day-trippers have departed—sunrise at popular spots offers both solitude and spectacular lighting. Focus on less-visited areas like Hetch Hetchy, Wawona, or the high country along Tioga Road instead of concentrating solely on Yosemite Valley. Even in the valley, trails like the Valley Loop, Pohono, or sections of the John Muir Trail see fewer hikers than the popular Mist Trail. Weekdays are significantly less crowded than weekends, and a midweek visit can feel like a different park altogether. Consider staying inside the park to enjoy early mornings and evenings when the natural rhythms of Yosemite are most evident.
