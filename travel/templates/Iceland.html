Iceland

Discover the land of fire and ice with its dramatic landscapes, powerful waterfalls, geothermal wonders, and vibrant culture.

About Iceland

Iceland, officially the Republic of Iceland, is a Nordic island country in the North Atlantic Ocean. With a population of just over 370,000, it's one of Europe's most sparsely populated countries. Known as the "Land of Fire and Ice," Iceland features a dramatic landscape with volcanoes, geysers, hot springs, lava fields, and massive glaciers. The island sits on the Mid-Atlantic Ridge where the North American and Eurasian tectonic plates meet, resulting in significant volcanic activity. Settled by Norse explorers in the 9th century, Iceland has preserved its Nordic heritage while developing a distinct culture. Its capital, Reykjavík, is the northernmost capital of a sovereign state and home to two-thirds of the country's population. Iceland is renowned for its commitment to renewable energy, with nearly all electricity produced from hydroelectric and geothermal sources. The nation consistently ranks among the top countries for quality of life, gender equality, and happiness. Despite its name and northerly location, Iceland's climate is milder than might be expected, thanks to the Gulf Stream. The country's unspoiled nature, breathtaking landscapes, and unique phenomena like the midnight sun and Northern Lights draw visitors from around the world.

Top Attractions

Blue Lagoon

Famous geothermal spa with milky-blue waters rich in minerals, set amid a dramatic lava field landscape.

Golden Circle

Popular tourist route featuring the powerful Gullfoss waterfall, Geysir geothermal area, and Þingvellir National Park.

Jökulsárlón

Stunning glacier lagoon filled with floating icebergs, adjacent to the famous Diamond Beach where ice chunks wash ashore.

Reykjavík

Iceland's vibrant capital featuring distinctive architecture, colorful buildings, innovative cuisine, and a dynamic arts scene.

Travel Tips

Essential information for your Icelandic adventure

Best Time to Visit

Summer (June-August) offers mild temperatures (10-15°C), nearly 24 hours of daylight, and ideal conditions for hiking and exploring. This is peak tourist season with higher prices. Winter (November-March) brings shorter days but creates perfect conditions for viewing the Northern Lights and experiencing winter activities like ice caving. Expect temperatures between -5°C and 5°C. The shoulder seasons of spring (April-May) and autumn (September-October) offer fewer crowds and reasonable weather, though some highland roads may be closed. Iceland's weather is famously changeable year-round—the local saying "if you don't like the weather, wait five minutes" holds true.

Transportation

Renting a car is the most popular and flexible way to explore Iceland, with the Ring Road (Route 1) circling the entire island. In winter, consider a 4x4 vehicle due to snow and ice. Iceland has no train system, but efficient buses connect major towns and tourist destinations. The Reykjavík area has good public transportation with the Strætó bus system. For remote areas, especially in the highlands, super jeep tours or specialized vehicles are necessary. Many travelers opt for guided tours, ranging from day trips from Reykjavík to multi-day expeditions. Domestic flights connect Reykjavík with larger towns like Akureyri and Ísafjörður, saving time when traveling longer distances. Always check road conditions before driving, especially in winter or when venturing into the highlands, at road.is.

Local Cuisine

Icelandic cuisine features fresh seafood, free-range lamb, and dairy products. Try traditional dishes like hákarl (fermented shark), skyr (yogurt-like dairy product), hangikjöt (smoked lamb), and plokkfiskur (fish stew). For adventurous eaters, specialties include svið (sheep's head) and lifrarpylsa (liver sausage). Modern Icelandic restaurants blend traditional ingredients with international techniques, creating an innovative food scene. Hot dogs (pylsur) are a popular street food—try one "with everything" at Bæjarins Beztu Pylsur in Reykjavík. Geothermal bakeries use natural heat to bake rúgbrauð (rye bread) underground. Alcohol is expensive, with beer only being legalized in 1989. Tap water in Iceland is some of the purest in the world—bring a reusable bottle and avoid buying bottled water.

Cultural Etiquette

Icelanders are direct, informal, and eco-conscious. When entering a residence, remove shoes. Service charges are included in invoices, thus tipping is not anticipated. Swimming pools are cultural institutions; shower sans swimwear before entering for hygiene. Follow "leave no trace" and stay on established trails to respect nature. Off-road driving is forbidden and harms sensitive ecosystems. Icelanders are proud of their language, so try some phrases. Most Icelanders speak English well. Take Icelanders' belief in huldufólk (hidden people) and elves as more than superstition. Icelanders cherish punctuality and communicate openly. Resistance to severe conditions has built community and resilience. Even in formal settings, Icelanders use first names. Weather is always discussed due to its changeability. Many companies are closed or have limited hours on Sunday, a family day.

Iceland travel advice

These questions are the most asked questions and are answerd here.

What are some self-drive tour itineraries in Iceland?

Popular self-drive itineraries include: 1) Golden Circle (1 day) - covering Þingvellir National Park, Geysir geothermal area, and Gullfoss waterfall in a 300km loop from Reykjavik. 2) South Coast (2-3 days) - featuring Seljalandsfoss and Skógafoss waterfalls, Reynisfjara black beach, and Jökulsárlón glacier lagoon. 3) Ring Road (7-10 days) - the complete 1,332km circuit visiting all regions including Akureyri, Lake Mývatn, and the East Fjords. 4) Snæfellsnes Peninsula (1-2 days) - exploring the "Iceland in miniature" peninsula with Kirkjufell mountain and black churches. 5) Westfjords (3-4 days) - for travelers seeking remote landscapes and fewer tourists. Best driving seasons are May-September; winter trips (October-April) should stick to main roads and require 4WD vehicles. Always check road conditions at road.is before departing.

What are the best accommodation options in Iceland?

Iceland accommodation options include: 1) Hotels - ranging from international chains in Reykjavik (€150-300/night) to boutique countryside hotels (€200-400/night). 2) Guesthouses - family-run options offering shared or private bathrooms (€100-200/night) and often including breakfast. 3) Hostels - budget-friendly dormitories (€30-50/bed) and private rooms (€80-120/night) in major towns. 4) Farm stays - authentic rural experiences (€120-180/night) with potential for horseback riding or farm activities. 5) Apartments/cottages - self-catering options ideal for families (€150-300/night). 6) Camping - permitted only at designated sites (€10-15/person) from June-September. Book accommodations 4-6 months in advance for summer (June-August) and 2-3 months ahead for other seasons. Options become extremely limited in small towns, particularly along the eastern Ring Road. Many properties require full payment in advance with strict cancellation policies.

What are notable stops on Iceland's Ring Road?

Key Ring Road (Route 1) stops include: South - Seljalandsfoss waterfall (walkable behind), Skógafoss waterfall, Dyrhólaey cliffs, Reynisfjara black sand beach, Fjaðrárgljúfur canyon, and Jökulsárlón glacier lagoon. East - Vestrahorn mountain, scenic East Fjords, and colorful Seyðisfjörður town. North - Lake Mývatn geothermal area, Dettifoss (Europe's most powerful waterfall), whale-watching town of Húsavík, and Akureyri (Iceland's "northern capital"). West - Hraunfossar lava waterfalls, Borgarnes historical museum, and Gerðuberg basalt columns. The complete 1,332km circuit requires minimum 7 days but 10-14 days is ideal. Summer (June-August) offers 24-hour daylight but crowded sites; May and September provide balance between accessibility and fewer tourists. Winter driving (October-April) often encounters road closures, particularly in the eastern and northern sections.

What are the payment methods in Iceland?

Iceland is essentially cashless: 1) Credit/debit cards are accepted almost everywhere, even for small purchases and at remote locations. Visa and Mastercard are universally accepted; American Express less so. 2) Contactless payment is widely available. 3) Mobile payments including Apple Pay and Google Pay work throughout the country. 4) For the few cash situations (small rural businesses, some toilets), the currency is Icelandic Króna (ISK). ATMs are available in towns but can be scarce in rural areas. Notify your bank before travel as suspicious foreign transactions may trigger card blocks. PIN numbers are required for credit card transactions. Currency exchange at Keflavík Airport offers reasonable rates if needed, but most travelers operate entirely without cash. Tipping is not expected or customary in restaurants or taxis, as service charges are included.

What are some recommended tour operators in Iceland?

Recommended tour operators include: 1) Arctic Adventures - adventure specialists offering glacier hikes, ice caves, and multi-day treks. 2) Reykjavik Excursions - runs the popular Airport Flybus and comprehensive Golden Circle tours. 3) GJ Travel - offers small-group super jeep and highland tours with local guides. 4) Extreme Iceland - focuses on off-the-beaten-path experiences and winter activities. 5) Guide to Iceland - an aggregator platform with customer reviews for comparing different operators. 6) Hey Iceland - specializes in farm stays and culturally immersive experiences. 7) Nordic Visitor - highly rated for self-drive tour packages with accommodations included. 8) Special Tours - known for whale watching excursions. For Northern Lights tours (September-March), choose operators offering free second attempts if lights aren't visible. Summer tours should be booked 2-3 months in advance; popular glacier cave tours (November-March) sell out 4+ months ahead.

What should I know about renting a car in Iceland?

Essential car rental information: 1) Local companies (Blue Car Rental, Lotus, Arctic) often offer better value than internationals (Hertz, Avis, Budget). 2) 2WD vehicles are sufficient for summer Ring Road trips on paved roads; 4WD is essential for F-roads (highlands) and winter driving. 3) Typical costs: €50-80/day for compact 2WD, €100-180/day for 4WD SUVs, €200-350/day for campervans. 4) Insurance options are crucial - Sand and Ash Protection (SAAP) and Gravel Protection are strongly recommended but not always included in basic coverage. 5) Drivers must be 20+ (23+ for larger vehicles) with 1+ year license history. 6) Fuel is expensive (€2.00-2.30/liter) and stations are scarce in rural areas; many require chip-and-PIN credit cards at unmanned pumps. 7) Off-road driving is strictly prohibited and heavily fined. 8) Always check road.is for conditions and safetravel.is for weather warnings before departures. Book 3-4 months ahead for summer rentals.

What are the bus transportation options in Iceland?

Iceland's bus options include: 1) Strætó - public bus service connecting major towns with limited schedules (1-2 daily departures) and affordable fares (Reykjavik-Akureyri around €70). 2) Reykjavik City Bus - local transportation in the capital area (single ride €4). 3) Airport transfers - Flybus and Airport Direct connect Keflavík Airport with Reykjavik (45 minutes, €25-30 one-way). 4) Hop-on/hop-off passes - summer-only bus passports by companies like Sterna Travel allow flexible travel along the Ring Road. 5) Highland buses - specialized 4WD services to Þórsmörk, Landmannalaugar, and other mountain areas from June-September requiring advance booking. Buses offer free WiFi and sometimes USB charging. The main limitation is frequency, with daily service on popular routes (Reykjavik-Vík-Höfn) during summer reducing to 3-4 weekly departures in winter, while remote areas may only have 1-2 weekly connections. Download the official Strætó app for schedules and tickets.

What are the must-see cultural attractions in Reykjavik?

Reykjavik cultural highlights include: 1) Hallgrímskirkja - iconic Lutheran church with observation tower offering city views (€10 entry fee). 2) Harpa Concert Hall - award-winning glass architecture hosting musical performances and exhibitions. 3) National Museum of Iceland - comprehensive history collection from settlement to modern times (€15 entry). 4) Settlement Exhibition - archaeological remains of a Viking longhouse with interactive displays. 5) Reykjavik Art Museum - spanning three locations with Icelandic and international modern art. 6) Perlan - nature museum with ice cave replica and excellent city viewpoint. 7) Árbær Open Air Museum - reconstructed historical buildings showing traditional Icelandic life. 8) Saga Museum - Viking history through realistic wax figures. 9) Street art throughout downtown, especially along Laugavegur and Skólavörðustígur. Most museums open 10am-5pm with extended summer hours and are walkable within downtown. The Reykjavik City Card (€35/24 hours) includes entry to most attractions plus public transportation.
