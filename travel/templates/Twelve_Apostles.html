The Twelve Apostles

Marvel at these magnificent limestone stacks rising majestically from the Southern Ocean along Victoria's dramatic Great Ocean Road coastline.

About the Twelve Apostles

The Twelve Apostles are a collection of limestone stacks rising dramatically from the Southern Ocean along Victoria's spectacular coastline. Located approximately 275 kilometers west of Melbourne in Port Campbell National Park, these iconic formations were created through a process of erosion beginning around 10-20 million years ago. The relentless force of the Southern Ocean gradually eroded the soft limestone cliffs, carving them into caves which eventually became arches. When these arches collapsed, they left the isolated pillars standing up to 45 meters high that we see today. Despite their name, there were never actually twelve stacks—at their peak, there were nine, though one collapsed in 2005, leaving eight remaining. The Twelve Apostles are part of a larger limestone coastline featuring other remarkable formations including Loch Ard Gorge, London Bridge (now London Arch after its connection to the mainland collapsed in 1990), and the Grotto. This stretch of coastline holds cultural significance for the traditional owners, the Eastern Maar peoples, who have inhabited the area for thousands of years. The traditional name for the area is Ngarringa, meaning "disappearing into the mouth of the sea." The natural sculptures continue to erode at a rate of approximately 2 centimeters per year, making them a constantly evolving natural wonder. The area was first known as "The Sow and Piglets" (Muttonbird Island being "The Sow" and the smaller limestone stacks "The Piglets") until the 1950s, when it was renamed to the more majestic "Twelve Apostles" to attract tourism—proving successful as they now draw over two million visitors annually.

Top Experiences

Travel Tips

Essential information for visiting the Twelve Apostles

Best Time to Visit

The Twelve Apostles can be visited year-round, but timing significantly impacts the experience. For the best lighting conditions, visit at sunrise (when fewer tourists are present) or sunset (more crowded but spectacular golden light). Weekdays are considerably less crowded than weekends. Weather-wise, November through April offers milder temperatures (15-25°C/59-77°F) and more stable conditions, though summer (December-February) brings peak tourist numbers. Winter (June-August) provides dramatic storm-watching opportunities with powerful waves crashing against the formations, but prepare for cold winds and potential rain. Shoulder seasons (March-May and September-November) offer a good balance of reasonable weather and fewer crowds. If your schedule is flexible, aim for mid-week visits outside school holiday periods. The most crowded hours are typically 11am-3pm when tour buses arrive from Melbourne. During peak season (December-January), arrive before 9am or after 4pm to avoid the largest crowds. The visitor facilities, including parking, viewing platforms, and restrooms, are accessible 24/7 with no entrance fee.

Getting There & Around

The Twelve Apostles are located 275km west of Melbourne (approximately 4-5 hours driving time). Most visitors arrive via the scenic Great Ocean Road, which follows the coastline and passes through charming towns like Lorne and Apollo Bay. Alternatively, the inland route via Princes Highway (A1) to Colac and then south to the coast is faster but less scenic. For those without a vehicle, organized day tours from Melbourne (AUD$100-150) provide transportation and often include other attractions along the Great Ocean Road, though these tours typically allow limited time at each stop. Public transport options are limited, but V/Line coaches connect Melbourne to Warrnambool with stops at Apollo Bay and Port Campbell, requiring additional taxi services to reach the Twelve Apostles. Once at the visitor center, the main viewing platform is accessible via a paved walkway and underpass beneath the Great Ocean Road. To explore nearby attractions like Loch Ard Gorge (3km west), Gibson Steps (1km east), and London Arch (7km west), a vehicle is essential as these sites are spread along the coastline with no connecting walkways or shuttles. The Twelve Apostles Visitor Facility includes a large parking area (free), restrooms, and basic information displays but no food services. For accommodation and meals, Port Campbell (10km west) offers the closest options.

Photography Tips

The Twelve Apostles offer exceptional photography opportunities with changing light conditions throughout the day. Morning light (sunrise to mid-morning) illuminates the eastern faces of the formations, while afternoon and sunset light bathes the western aspects in golden hues. For classic postcard shots, the main viewing platform provides excellent vantage points, particularly the western end of the boardwalk for unobstructed views. A wide-angle lens (16-35mm equivalent) helps capture the expansive coastal scenery, while a telephoto lens (70-200mm) allows close-up details of individual formations and wildlife. Landscape orientation works best for capturing multiple stacks, while portrait orientation emphasizes their height against the ocean. A polarizing filter reduces glare from the water and enhances the blue sky, while graduated neutral density filters help balance bright skies with darker foregrounds. Tripods are essential for low-light photography at sunrise/sunset and for creating long exposures that smooth the ocean surface (bring a remote shutter release for best results). For unique perspectives, consider Gibson Steps beach access (when open) for ground-level views looking up at the stacks, helicopter tours for aerial compositions, or the lesser-known Gibsons Beach viewing area. Weather conditions significantly impact photography—partly cloudy days often provide the most dramatic lighting with sunbeams piercing through clouds. During winter, stormy conditions create dramatic wave action and moody atmospheres worth capturing. Be prepared for strong winds that can cause camera shake and salt spray that may damage equipment—bring lens cleaning supplies and protective gear.

Practical Information

The Twelve Apostles site is open 24/7 with no entrance fees, making it accessible regardless of when you visit. The visitor center facilities include restrooms, parking, and informational displays but no food services or accommodation. The nearest town with services is Port Campbell (10km west), offering restaurants, accommodation, and a small grocery store. While the main viewing platforms are wheelchair accessible via paved paths, some of the surrounding attractions have limited accessibility due to stairs or uneven terrain. Weather conditions can change rapidly along this exposed coastline—dress in layers and bring wind/rain protection even on seemingly clear days. Strong winds are common year-round, and the viewing platforms offer no shelter. Mobile phone coverage is generally reliable at the main visitor center but can be patchy along stretches of the Great Ocean Road. Safety concerns include staying on designated paths and viewing platforms, as the limestone cliffs are unstable and dangerous. Never attempt to climb down unmarked areas or cross safety barriers. Gibson Steps beach access is occasionally closed during high tides or dangerous sea conditions. For a more comprehensive experience, consider staying overnight in Port Campbell or Apollo Bay rather than attempting to rush the entire Great Ocean Road as a day trip from Melbourne. The closest fuel stations are in Port Campbell and Princetown, so plan accordingly. Allow at least 1-2 hours to fully appreciate the Twelve Apostles and immediate surroundings, or 3-4 hours if including nearby attractions like Loch Ard Gorge and London Arch.

Twelve Apostles FAQ

These questions are the most asked questions and are answerd here.

How many Apostles are actually left standing?

Despite the name "Twelve Apostles," there were never actually twelve limestone stacks at this location. When the site was officially named in the 1950s (previously called "The Sow and Piglets"), there were nine main stacks visible from the viewing platforms. In July 2005, one of the most prominent 45-meter tall stacks collapsed dramatically into the sea, reducing the count to eight. Then in 2009, the "Island Archway" formation collapsed, creating a new stack nicknamed "Two Apostles," though this formation is not typically counted among the main Apostles. Currently, eight major stacks remain that are considered part of the Twelve Apostles collection. The discrepancy between the name and actual number is purely a marketing decision—tourism authorities believed "Twelve Apostles" sounded more impressive and biblically significant than the original name. The limestone coast continues to erode at an estimated rate of about 2cm per year, so the formations are constantly changing. Geologists predict that new Apostles will continue to form over thousands of years as the current cliffs are worn away by the powerful Southern Ocean. Interestingly, underwater research conducted in 2016 discovered an additional five limestone stacks submerged beneath the ocean near the existing formations, suggesting that erosion has been reshaping this coastline for much longer than previously thought.

Is it better to see the Twelve Apostles from Melbourne or Adelaide?

While the Twelve Apostles can be accessed from either Melbourne or Adelaide, Melbourne is generally the preferred starting point for most visitors. From Melbourne, the Twelve Apostles are approximately 275km away (4-5 hours driving) with two route options: the world-famous Great Ocean Road coastal route through towns like Torquay, Lorne, and Apollo Bay, offering spectacular ocean views and rainforest scenery; or the faster inland route via Princes Highway and Colac (3.5 hours). From Adelaide, the distance is considerably greater at around 550km (7-8 hours driving), making it challenging as a day trip. However, starting from Adelaide allows you to approach the formations from the west, visiting less crowded attractions like Bay of Islands and London Arch first, before reaching the Twelve Apostles. Melbourne offers more tour options, including one-day tours (though rushed), two-day tours with overnight stays, and helicopter tours. Adelaide-based tours typically require 2-3 days and often include other regions like the Grampians National Park. If you have limited time, Melbourne is the more practical choice. For a more leisurely experience that avoids backtracking, consider a one-way journey starting in Melbourne and ending in Adelaide (or vice versa), allowing you to drive the entire 650km coastal route at a relaxed pace over 3-5 days. Accommodation availability and rental car drop-off fees should be factored into planning this option. Ultimately, the Great Ocean Road itself is arguably as impressive as the Twelve Apostles destination, making the Melbourne-based approach more scenic and varied.

What are the best stops along the Great Ocean Road to the Twelve Apostles?

The 243km journey along the Great Ocean Road from Torquay to the Twelve Apostles offers numerous worthwhile stops. Starting from Melbourne, visit Bells Beach near Torquay, world-famous for surfing competitions and dramatic cliffs. The Memorial Arch at Eastern View marks the official start of the Great Ocean Road, commemorating soldiers who built the road after World War I. Lorne, a charming seaside town about 2 hours from Melbourne, offers excellent beaches, waterfall hikes at Erskine Falls, and quality dining options. The Kennett River Koala Walk provides reliable wild koala sightings in eucalyptus trees along Grey River Road. Apollo Bay marks roughly the halfway point and is ideal for lunch, with excellent seafood restaurants and beaches. Approaching the Apostles, detour inland to the Otway National Park for Maits Rest Rainforest Walk, a 30-minute boardwalk through ancient temperate rainforest, or the nearby Otway Fly Treetop Adventures for canopy walks and zip-lining. Cape Otway Lightstation, Australia's oldest surviving lighthouse, offers panoramic ocean views and historical exhibits. After the Twelve Apostles, continue west to Loch Ard Gorge (3 minutes' drive) to explore dramatic coastal formations and learn about the famous 1878 shipwreck. London Arch (formerly London Bridge before its connecting span collapsed in 1990) and The Grotto are additional limestone formations within 10 minutes of the Apostles. The Bay of Islands, 30 minutes west of the Apostles near Peterborough, offers similar coastal scenery with far fewer tourists. For wildlife enthusiasts, Tower Hill Wildlife Reserve near Warrnambool provides encounters with emus, kangaroos, and koalas in an extinct volcano setting.

Are the Twelve Apostles worth visiting as a day trip from Melbourne?

While the Twelve Apostles can be visited as a day trip from Melbourne, doing so involves significant trade-offs that travelers should consider. A day trip requires 8-9 hours of driving time round-trip (4-5 hours each way), leaving limited time to actually enjoy the attractions. Organized coach tours (AUD$100-150) offer convenience but typically allow only 30-45 minutes at the Twelve Apostles themselves, with brief stops at Loch Ard Gorge and perhaps one or two other locations. Self-driving provides more flexibility but can be exhausting, especially when returning along the winding Great Ocean Road after a full day of sightseeing. For photography enthusiasts, a day trip rarely allows for optimal lighting conditions at the Apostles, which are best at sunrise or sunset. Weather unpredictability along the coast means day-trippers risk arriving during poor visibility or rain with no flexibility to wait for conditions to improve. Most visitors who opt for a day trip report wishing they had allocated more time. For those with limited schedules, a day trip can provide a basic experience of this iconic landmark, but consider the focused inland route (via Princes Highway) at least one way to reduce driving time. A better alternative is a minimum one-night stay in Apollo Bay, Port Campbell, or Warrnambool, allowing for a more leisurely exploration over two days. The optimal experience involves 2-3 days exploring the entire Great Ocean Road region, staying overnight in coastal towns to experience sunset and sunrise at key locations. For those with only one day available, helicopter tours from Melbourne (around AUD$550 per person) provide a time-efficient alternative, offering aerial views of the Apostles and coast in a 3-4 hour round trip.

Which is better: the Twelve Apostles or the Great Ocean Road?

The question of whether the Twelve Apostles or the Great Ocean Road journey is better represents a classic destination versus journey comparison. The Twelve Apostles provide a concentrated, iconic Australian landmark experience with dramatic limestone formations that impress even with brief viewing. The site offers impressive infrastructure with accessible boardwalks and viewing platforms suitable for visitors of all mobility levels. However, the Apostles experience can be diminished by large crowds (over 2 million annual visitors), particularly during peak hours when dozens of tour buses arrive simultaneously. In contrast, the Great Ocean Road journey offers varied experiences across 243 kilometers of scenic coastline, including pristine beaches, charming seaside towns, ancient rainforest walks, wildlife encounters, and numerous less-crowded limestone formations. This diversity allows travelers to customize their experience based on interests (beaches, hiking, food, wildlife) and avoid the worst crowds by visiting alternate sites. The Road's winding coastal sections between Lorne and Apollo Bay are considered among the world's most scenic drives. Most travelers who allocate sufficient time (at least two days) rate the complete Great Ocean Road experience higher than just the Apostles, noting that while the Apostles are impressive, they represent just one highlight along a remarkable journey. Travelers consistently report that rushing to the Apostles without experiencing the journey leaves them feeling they've missed the essence of the region. For those with limited time, the inland route directly to the Apostles provides efficiency, but those with flexibility should consider the entire Road experience, with the Apostles as the culminating highlight. Ideally, experience both—the journey and the destination—by allowing 2-3 days to explore the region properly.

Where should I stay when visiting the Twelve Apostles?

Several locations offer convenient accommodation options when visiting the Twelve Apostles, each with different advantages. Port Campbell, just 10 minutes' drive from the Apostles, provides the closest accommodations and ideal positioning for sunrise or sunset visits. This small town offers a range of options from the upscale Southern Ocean Villas (AUD$250-350) to the mid-range Port Campbell Parkview Motel (AUD$150-200) and budget-friendly Port Campbell Hostel (AUD$30-90). The town has several quality restaurants and a peaceful harbor setting. Apollo Bay, located about 1.5 hours east of the Apostles, offers a larger selection of accommodations and restaurants in a scenic setting. Options include luxury beach houses (AUD$300-500), the well-regarded Apollo Bay Motel (AUD$150-250), and the Apollo Bay Eco YHA (AUD$35-120). While further from the Apostles, it's well-positioned for exploring the eastern section of the Great Ocean Road. Warrnambool, 1 hour west of the Apostles, is the largest city in the region with extensive services, accommodation options in all price ranges, and attractions including the Flagstaff Hill Maritime Village and whale watching (May-October). For a unique experience, consider the boutique Alkina Lodge (AUD$450-650) near Princetown, offering luxury eco-friendly accommodation just 20 minutes from the Apostles in a bushland setting with wildlife viewing opportunities. Farmstays and B&Bs in the Otway hinterland provide a rural Australian experience within 30-45 minutes of the Apostles—Wombalano Country Retreat (AUD$200-250) and Otway Escapes (AUD$220-300) are well-reviewed options. Camping is available at Cumberland River Holiday Park near Lorne, Bimbi Park Cape Otway (with koalas overhead), and Princetown Recreation Reserve (closest campground to the Apostles). Accommodation throughout the region books quickly during peak season (December-February) and holiday periods, with many properties requiring two-night minimum stays on weekends.

What other attractions are near the Twelve Apostles?

The coastal area surrounding the Twelve Apostles features numerous natural attractions worth exploring. Within a 10-minute drive, Loch Ard Gorge offers dramatic cliff formations, a sheltered beach, and the compelling history of an 1878 shipwreck where only two teenagers survived. Nearby, The Arch, London Arch (formerly London Bridge), and The Grotto present unique limestone formations with their own distinct character and fewer crowds. Gibson Steps, just 1km east of the Apostles, provides beach access where visitors can experience the towering cliffs and sea stacks from sea level when conditions permit. The Bay of Islands, Bay of Martyrs, and Halladale Point, located 15-30 minutes west of the Apostles near Peterborough, offer similar coastal scenery with significantly smaller crowds. For wildlife enthusiasts, the 12 Apostles Marine National Park protects the underwater environment around the formations, with boat tours available from Port Campbell showcasing marine life and providing unique perspectives of the cliffs from the ocean. Inland from the coast, Otway National Park offers temperate rainforest experiences including Maits Rest and Melba Gully with ancient trees, glow worms, and waterfalls. Cape Otway Lighthouse, Australia's oldest working lighthouse, stands on dramatic cliffs about an hour east of the Apostles. Adventure activities in the region include the Otway Fly Treetop Walk and Zipline, offering canopy-level views of the rainforest. Tower Hill Wildlife Reserve near Warrnambool provides almost guaranteed encounters with wild kangaroos, emus, and koalas in an extinct volcano crater. The historic fishing village of Port Fairy, an hour west of the Apostles, features well-preserved 19th-century buildings, excellent dining, and a vibrant arts scene. For cheese lovers, Timboon Dairy and Apostle Whey Cheese offer local cheese tasting experiences within 20-30 minutes of the Apostles. The Great Ocean Walk, a 100km hiking trail from Apollo Bay to the Twelve Apostles, provides access to secluded beaches and coastal viewpoints inaccessible by car.
